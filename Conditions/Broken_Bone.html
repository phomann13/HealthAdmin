Broken Bone

What is a Broken Bone?

A broken bone, also known as a fracture, occurs when there is a break or crack in the continuity of a bone. It typically happens due to trauma or excessive force applied to the bone, such as a fall, sports injury, or car accident. Bones can break in various ways, including complete fractures where the bone separates into two or more pieces, or incomplete fractures where the bone is cracked but still partially connected. The severity of a broken bone can range from minor hairline fractures to more severe compound fractures where the bone pierces through the skin.

What are the Symtpoms of a Broken Bone?

Symptoms of a broken bone include intense pain, swelling, bruising, deformity or angulation of the affected area, difficulty moving or bearing weight, and sometimes a grinding or popping sound at the time of injury. In some cases, an open fracture may be accompanied by bleeding and visible bone protrusion. These symptoms can vary depending on the location and type of fracture.

Where should You receive treatment?

The choice of medical care for a broken bone depends on the severity of the symptoms. For minor fractures or suspected fractures with relatively mild symptoms, patients can often start by visiting a primary care doctor. Primary care physicians can assess the injury, order X-rays if necessary, provide initial treatment such as immobilization or splinting, and refer patients to a specialist if needed. Urgent care centers can also be an option for less severe fractures, as they can provide immediate care, including X-rays and casting. However, for severe fractures, multiple fractures, open fractures, or fractures accompanied by significant pain, bleeding, or neurovascular compromise, it is crucial to go to an emergency room. Emergency rooms have the necessary resources, including advanced imaging and surgical capabilities, to provide urgent and specialized care for severe fractures.
